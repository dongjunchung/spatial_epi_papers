## Spatio-temporal brucellosis clustering in North Khorasan, Iran

**Paper:** Niloofar Sadooghi, Mohammad Maroosi, Fatemeh Khorashadizadeh,
Ayoub Fathabadi, Hamidreza Shoraka, and Maryam Dadar. "Spatio-temporal
analysis of human brucellosis in North Khorasan, Iran (2015-2023): A
cross-sectional study." One Health, 2026.

**Publication date:** June 10, 2026. Newly indexed in PubMed on June 22, 2026
at 13:43 UTC.

**Source:** [PubMed PMID: 42328501](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42328501/);
[PMC full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13279910/);
[doi:10.1016/j.onehlt.2026.101474](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.onehlt.2026.101474)

**Modeling approach:** The study analyzes 2,855 confirmed human brucellosis
cases from 2015-2023 in North Khorasan province. The abstract reports ArcGIS
10.8.2 for spatial analyses, logistic regression for exposure associations,
and Global and Local Moran's I with LISA cluster maps for spatial clustering.
The report uses explanatory notation for county-year incidence and spatial
weights because the abstract does not provide the exact equations or spatial
weight definition.

**Key finding:** The abstract reports substantial spatial clustering of
brucellosis incidence. Bojnord, Shirvan, and Faruj were persistent
high-incidence clusters from 2016-2023, with Shirvan and Faruj confirmed as
persistent high-high LISA clusters. Rural residence was associated with higher
odds of unpasteurized dairy consumption (OR 2.54, 95% CI 1.91-3.36) and
livestock interaction (OR 8.93, 95% CI 6.77-11.83); family history of
brucellosis was associated with cattle exposure (OR 2.13, 95% CI 1.40-3.33).

**Why it matters:** The paper turns routine case surveillance into stable and
intermittent hotspot signals that can guide county-specific brucellosis
control, including livestock vaccination, dairy-safety education, and targeted
surveillance in persistent clusters.

**Caution on details:** The technical schematic is based on the PubMed abstract
and PMC metadata available during this run. It labels Global Moran's I, Local
Moran's I, LISA, and logistic-regression components reported by the source, but
uses generic notation where the abstract does not state formulas, covariate
sets, or spatial weights.

## Climate drivers of malaria risk in children under five

**Paper:** Manuel Martellini O Nocentini, S. Pandey, L. Olivetti,
F. Defilippo, M. Wybrant, K. Worou, M. Mazzoleni, M. Quiros-Roldan,
P. Byakika, C. Torti, C. Magnusson, A. Gasparrini, G. Messori, and
E. Raffetti. "Climatic Drivers of Malaria risk in Children Under Five: A
Large-Scale Analysis of individual-level data for 350,000 children in 26
Sub-Saharan African Countries." medRxiv preprint, 2026.

**Publication date:** Posted June 22, 2026 in the medRxiv API result for the
2026-06-21 to 2026-06-22 interval.

**Source:** [doi:10.64898/2026.06.17.26355855](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.17.26355855);
[medRxiv source XML](https://www.medrxiv.org/content/early/2026/06/22/2026.06.17.26355855.source.xml)

**Modeling approach:** The preprint links Demographic and Health Survey malaria
test outcomes for children aged 5-59 months in 26 Sub-Saharan African countries
from 2006-2023 to high-resolution climate exposures: temperature,
precipitation, soil moisture, actual evapotranspiration, and specific humidity.
It reports mixed-effect logistic regression with distributed lag non-linear
models over a 1-6 month lag window, controlling for seasonality and long-term
trends, and assessing effect modification by maternal education, household
wealth, residence, water source, sanitation, child age and sex, insecticide-
treated net use, and household-head age.

**Key finding:** The abstract reports malaria prevalence of 19.5%. Estimated
risk was highest near 24 C for temperature (OR 1.45, 95% CI 1.36-1.54), rose
with precipitation up to roughly 59-120 mm (OR 1.10, 95% CI 1.07-1.12), rose
with soil moisture up to about 80 mm (OR 1.11, 95% CI 1.08-1.14), increased
near-linearly with evapotranspiration, and declined at higher specific humidity
levels, reaching OR 0.55 (95% CI 0.49-0.61) at 17 g/kg. Low and moderate
temperature risks were especially evident among children not sleeping under an
insecticide-treated net.

**Why it matters:** The paper provides a large-scale, individual-level
environmental exposure modeling template for translating climate histories into
lag-specific malaria risk signals. Those signals can support early warning,
timed vector-control planning, and targeted prevention among children with
lower protection.

**Caution on details:** The technical schematic uses generic DLNM and mixed-
effects notation because only the abstract and medRxiv API metadata were used
for this update. Exact spline bases, random-effect structure, geocoding rules,
and validation design should be checked in the full preprint before
implementation.
