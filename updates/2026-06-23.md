## Memory mechanisms for behavioural change in Bayesian spatial epidemic models

**Paper:** Yicheng Mao, Rob Deardon, and Lorna E. Deeth. "Memory mechanisms for
behavioural change in Bayesian individual-level spatial epidemic models."
Infectious Disease Modelling, 2026.

**Publication date:** Published online June 2, 2026; newly indexed in PubMed on
June 22, 2026 at 13:42 UTC, after the previous automation run.

**Source:** [PubMed PMID: 42325476](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42325476/);
[PMC full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13276345/);
[doi:10.1016/j.idm.2026.05.008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.idm.2026.05.008)

**Modeling approach:** The paper extends Bayesian individual-level spatial
epidemic models with memory-enhanced behavioural change mechanisms. The source
reports four risk-perception specifications: memoryless, sliding-window,
power-law decay, and exponential decay memory. The spatial SIR individual-level
model likelihood is written over newly infected and still-susceptible
individuals at each time step, and inference is performed with an adaptive
random-walk Metropolis-Hastings sampler. Simulation experiments vary short and
long memory settings; the empirical application uses the 2001 U.K. foot-and-
mouth disease epidemic.

**Key finding:** The abstract reports that MEBC-ILMs can recover key
transmission and behavioural parameters under different memory settings and
remain predictively robust even when the assumed memory structure differs from
the true process. A basic behavioural-change ILM can perform poorly when memory
affects current behaviour but is not modeled.

**Why it matters:** Behavioural feedback is a major source of model error in
outbreak analysis. This paper gives spatial epidemic modelers a Bayesian route
for estimating how long past epidemic information continues to influence
contact-reducing behaviour.

**Caution on details:** The schematic uses equations visible in the PMC full
text for the reported memory functions and likelihood structure, but simplifies
typography and labels for readability.

## Spatiotemporal dengue trends in Bangladesh

**Paper:** Afsana Khan, Nazmul Sharif, Nadim Sharif, and Shuvra Kanti Dey.
"Spatiotemporal trends, evolving seasonality and symptoms of dengue in
Bangladesh, 2019-2025." New Microbes and New Infections, 2026.

**Publication date:** Published online June 11, 2026; newly indexed in PubMed
on June 22, 2026 at 13:43 UTC, after the previous automation run.

**Source:** [PubMed PMID: 42327490](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42327490/);
[PMC full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13277491/);
[doi:10.1016/j.nmni.2026.101793](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nmni.2026.101793)

**Modeling approach:** The study analyzes Bangladesh national dengue
surveillance data from 2000-2025 and environmental factors from the World Bank
Climate Portal and national meteorological databases. It compares prevalence,
incidence, rural-to-urban distribution, disease prognosis, seasonality, and
environmental associations between 2000-2018 and 2019-2025. The abstract
reports standardized incidence ratios, rural-to-urban incidence ratios, and
correlations with temperature, humidity, and rainfall.

**Key finding:** The abstract reports a sharp post-2019 increase in rural and
semi-urban dengue transmission. Rural-to-urban incidence ratios rose from
0.11-0.41 in 2000-2018 to 0.86-2.12 in 2019-2025. Seasonal peaks shifted
toward October-November, January-June incidence was significantly higher after
2019, and climate associations slightly strengthened in the post-2019 period
(reported correlation greater than 0.5). Available fatal-case records indicated
rapid clinical deterioration within 24-72 hours after diagnosis among a subset
of fatal cases.

**Why it matters:** The paper supports a practical surveillance message:
dengue control in Bangladesh can no longer be designed around historical urban
hotspots and older seasonal timing alone. Rural and semi-urban surveillance,
late-season vector control, and linked virologic/serologic monitoring become
more important.

**Caution on details:** The schematic uses generic formulas for standardized
incidence and climate correlation because the abstract does not provide full
model equations or denominator definitions.

## Geostatistical Toxoplasma gondii exposure mapping in Salvador, Brazil

**Paper:** Max T. Eyre, Joyce Y. Wang, Ianei de O. Carneiro, Renato B. Reis,
Elsio A. Wunder Jr., Nivison N. Junior, Guilherme S. Ribeiro, Fabio N. Souza,
Juliet O. Santana, Ellie A. Delight, Ridalva D. M. Felzemburgh, Francisco S.
Santana, Sharif Mohr, Astrid X. T. O. Melendez, Adriano Queiroz, Andreia C.
Santos, Jaqueline S. Cruz, Meghan Owens, Bruno Martorelli Di Genova, Mitermayer
G. Reis, Claudia Munoz-Zanzi, Peter J. Diggle, Federico Costa, and Albert I.
Ko. "Social marginalisation, environmental degradation and Toxoplasma gondii
exposure in urban informal settlements in Brazil." PLOS Neglected Tropical
Diseases, 2026.

**Publication date:** Published June 22, 2026; newly indexed in PubMed on June
22, 2026 at 18:59 UTC.

**Source:** [PubMed PMID: 42330015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42330015/);
[PLOS article](https://journals.plos.org/plosntds/article?id=10.1371/journal.pntd.0014453);
[doi:10.1371/journal.pntd.0014453](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pntd.0014453)

**Modeling approach:** The study uses archived samples from a cross-sectional
serosurvey of 728 children and adolescents aged 4-18 years in an urban
informal settlement in Salvador, Brazil. It combines Bayesian serocatalytic
models for sex-specific force of infection, regression analyses of demographic,
socioeconomic, behavioural, household, and environmental risk factors, and
geostatistical modeling of fine-scale spatial heterogeneity in seroprevalence.

**Key finding:** Overall seroprevalence was 49%. The Bayesian serocatalytic
models estimated sex-specific forces of infection of 0.078 for males and 0.050
for females. Seropositivity was associated with male sex, lower household
income, cat ownership, lower elevation, greater distance from the main road,
and reported sewer-water contact. However, 77.3% of seropositive participants
did not live in households with cats. Geostatistical modeling found clustered
hotspots exceeding 50-60% predicted prevalence, and covariate adjustment
attenuated but did not eliminate spatial clustering.

**Why it matters:** This is a strong environmental exposure modeling example:
it links serology, household conditions, terrain/road context, and latent
spatial structure to show that T. gondii exposure in informal settlements is
not explained only by domestic cat ownership. The estimated female
susceptibility at childbearing age also connects the spatial exposure surface
to congenital toxoplasmosis prevention.

**Caution on details:** The figure uses generic geostatistical notation for the
latent spatial process because the report uses the abstract and article
metadata rather than reproducing the full model specification.

## Dengue and chikungunya transmission in Kinshasa, DRC

**Paper:** Rachel Sendor, Jessie K. Edwards, Kristin Banek, Kashamuka
Mwandagalirwa, Justin Lessler, Daniel O. Espinoza, Izabella Castillo, Fabien
Vulu, Nono M. Muvamba, Joseph Alexandre Mavungu Bala, Marthe Nkalani, Georges
Kihuma, Joseph Losoma Atibu, Tommy Mambulu Nseka, Kyaw Lay Thwai, Lakshmanane
Premkumar, Rhoel R. Dinglasan, Michael Emch, Jonathan J. Juliano, Matthew
Collins, Antoinette Kitoto Tshefu, and Jonathan Parr. "Dengue and chikungunya
virus transmission in Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of the Congo." medRxiv
preprint, 2026.

**Publication date:** Posted June 22, 2026. The source XML reports a created
timestamp of June 22, 2026 at 06:45:34 -07:00, which is 13:45:34 UTC, after the
previous automation run.

**Source:** [doi:10.64898/2026.06.11.26355423](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.11.26355423);
[medRxiv source XML](https://www.medrxiv.org/content/early/2026/06/22/2026.06.11.26355423.source.xml)

**Modeling approach:** The preprint measures DENV and CHIKV IgG
seroprevalence in Kinshasa Province using antigen-capture ELISA on dried blood
spots collected in 2021 from 1,250 participants. It estimates force of
infection from age-stratified seroprevalence using Bayesian catalytic modeling.
The abstract reports that time-varying FOI models provided the best fit and
that spatial variation was detected.

**Key finding:** DENV IgG seroprevalence was 38.1% (95% CI 34.5%-41.8%) and
was highest in peri-urban Kimpoko sites at 54.9%. CHIKV IgG seroprevalence was
24.2% (95% CI 21.1%-27.6%), comparable between peri-urban Kimpoko and rural Bu,
with few seropositives in the city-center. DENV-CHIKV IgG co-occurrence was
detected in 12.8% of participants.

**Why it matters:** The study uses age-serology and catalytic FOI modeling to
turn sparse arbovirus surveillance into evidence of sustained DENV and CHIKV
circulation in the DRC. The spatial variation suggests that peri-urban and
rural surveillance may be as important as city-center monitoring.

**Caution on details:** The schematic uses generic catalytic-model notation
because only the abstract and source XML were reviewed in this run. Exact
priors, temporal basis functions, and spatial random-effect parameterization
should be checked in the full preprint before implementation.
