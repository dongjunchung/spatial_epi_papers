## Meteorology-linked infectious diarrhea risk and warning in China

**Paper:** Qiang Hao, Baofa Jiang, Yanjun Wang, Xiaodong Zhang, and Lin Zhang.
"Impact of meteorological factors on other infectious diarrhea in mainland
China: comprehensive risk assessment, forecasting and early warning." Journal
of Environmental Management, 2026.

**Publication date:** Published online June 24, 2026; newly entered in PubMed
on June 24, 2026 at 18:10 UTC, after the previous automation run.

**Source:** [PubMed PMID: 42341678](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42341678/);
[doi:10.1016/j.jenvman.2026.130303](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jenvman.2026.130303)

**Modeling approach:** The abstract reports a unified surveillance and
meteorology dataset for 316 mainland Chinese cities from 2014-2019. The study
uses an IPCC-style hazard-exposure-vulnerability framework, a quasi-Poisson
distributed lag non-linear model (DLNM), multivariate meta-analysis, spatial
mapping of hazard-exposure, sensitivity, adaptability, and vulnerability, and
forecast comparisons among SARIMAX, GAM, and random forest models. The early
warning system uses an RF forecast and the historical weekly-case 75th
percentile as the alert threshold.

**Key finding:** High-risk cities were persistently concentrated in Southern,
Jiangnan, and Southwest China. Random forest had the best reported forecasting
performance among the compared models, with test R2 of 0.76, RMSE of 41.44,
MAE of 31.39, representative-city observed-predicted R2 values greater than
0.974, and early-warning sensitivity of 94.69%, specificity of 86.18%, and AUC
of 0.918.

**Why it matters:** This paper links spatial climate-sensitive disease risk
assessment to operational weekly alerts, making it useful for climate-resilient
control of weather-sensitive infectious diseases at city scale.

**Caution on details:** The schematic uses generic DLNM and forecasting
notation because the PubMed abstract gives model classes and performance
metrics but not the full spline bases, covariate set, city-level random effects,
or validation split details.

## Mainland China Talaromyces marneffei spatiotemporal detection

**Paper:** Xinchang Lun, Jiale Yuan, Wenyin Qiao, Min Wang, Pei Li, Xi Wang,
Ronghua Jin, Jianguo Xu, Cao Chen, Rui Song, and Yamin Sun. "Epidemiology and
factors associated with Talaromyces marneffei detection in the mainland of
China, 2022-2024: a cross-sectional study of hospitalized patients." BMC Public
Health, 2026.

**Publication date:** Published online June 24, 2026; newly entered in PubMed
on June 25, 2026 at 00:28 UTC.

**Source:** [PubMed PMID: 42343343](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42343343/);
[doi:10.1186/s12889-026-28001-z](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12889-026-28001-z)

**Modeling approach:** The study analyzes targeted next-generation sequencing
data from patients hospitalized for acute respiratory tract infections from
2022-2024. It reports epidemiological, spatiotemporal, and pathogen
co-detection analyses, and uses geographical detector models with q-statistics
to quantify how meteorological, host-distribution, and social factors explain
spatial variance in T. marneffei detection risk. Bivariate detector analysis
evaluates whether paired factors explain more spatial variance than single
factors.

**Key finding:** The abstract reports 2,316 cases, significant spatial
clustering, bimodal seasonal peaks, highest susceptibility among males and
people aged 41-50, and Pneumocystis jirovecii as the predominant co-detected
pathogen. Dew point temperature had the strongest univariate explanatory power
for detection risk at 48.55%. Bivariate combinations generally enhanced
explanatory power, with average air pressure increasing substantially when
paired with other variables despite explaining only 1.4% alone.

**Why it matters:** The study extends T. marneffei surveillance beyond
HIV-positive or historically endemic groups by mapping heterogeneous
population-scale detection risk and environmental correlates across mainland
China.

**Caution on details:** The schematic uses generic geographical-detector
notation because the abstract identifies q-statistics and bivariate
interactions but not the exact strata definitions, spatial units, or all factor
layers.

## Amortized Bayesian areal wombling for disease boundaries

**Paper:** Kyle Lin Wu and Sudipto Banerjee. "Model-based Detection of Spatial
Disease Boundaries Using Amortized Bayesian Inference." medRxiv preprint,
2026.

**Publication date:** Posted June 24, 2026. The medRxiv source XML reports a
created timestamp of June 24, 2026 at 08:52 -07:00, which is 15:52 UTC, after
the previous automation run.

**Source:** [doi:10.64898/2026.06.21.26356187](https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.21.26356187);
[medRxiv source XML](https://www.medrxiv.org/content/early/2026/06/24/2026.06.21.26356187.source.xml)

**Modeling approach:** The abstract reports neural posterior estimation
embedded in a Bayesian areal wombling framework to accelerate spatial health
disparity detection between neighboring U.S. counties. The paper introduces a
Residual Disparity Elimination Target, defined as the reduction in mortality
or prevalence needed for a region to eliminate a significant disparity with a
neighbor. The application analyzes tracheal, bronchus, and lung cancer
mortality rates across mainland U.S. counties, with publicly available county
covariates including physical inactivity, health insurance prevalence,
unemployment, adult diabetes prevalence, adult obesity prevalence, social
vulnerability, smoking prevalence, and cancer mortality.

**Key finding:** The abstract reports concordance with MCMC analysis while
scaling areal wombling to hundreds of outcomes and translating boundary
evidence into interpretable policy objectives through the residual disparity
target.

**Why it matters:** Bayesian boundary detection is directly relevant to
disease mapping, but MCMC can limit repeated surveillance and multi-outcome
screening. Amortized inference could make posterior boundary probabilities and
policy-target calculations more scalable.

**Caution on details:** The schematic uses explanatory notation for the areal
likelihood, boundary probability, and neural posterior estimator because the
available source XML exposes only the abstract and data-availability statement.
Notation may differ from the preprint.
